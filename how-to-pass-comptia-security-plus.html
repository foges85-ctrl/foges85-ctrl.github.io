How to Pass CompTIA Security+

A realistic study plan for SY0-701, what the exam actually tests, how long to study, which resources are worth your time, and the strategies that make a real difference on exam day.

‚ö° The short version
CompTIA Security+ is a single exam (SY0-701) covering threats and vulnerabilities, architecture, implementation, operations, and governance. Most people pass with 2‚Äì4 months of consistent study. Security+ is scenario-heavy ‚Äî it doesn't ask you to define terms, it puts you in a situation and asks what you would do. Candidates who only memorise definitions fail. Candidates who understand how attacks work and how defences counter them pass.

üìä Security+ vs Network+ ‚Äî what's actually different

Network+ tests whether you understand how networks are built and how traffic moves. Security+ tests whether you understand how those networks are attacked, defended, and governed. There's meaningful overlap ‚Äî Security+ expects you to know networking fundamentals ‚Äî but the focus shifts from "how does it work" to "how is it exploited and protected."

If you have Network+, you already have the foundation. Ports, protocols, firewalls, and VPNs are review. The new material is threat intelligence, identity and access management, cryptography, cloud security, incident response, and governance frameworks ‚Äî give these areas the most time.

What the Security+ exam actually tests

SY0-701 has five domains. Note that threats, attacks, and vulnerabilities combined with security architecture make up nearly half the exam ‚Äî scenario-based thinking in these areas is where most candidates win or lose:

‚ö° The domain most candidates under-study

Security Program Management & Oversight at 20% ‚Äî risk management, compliance frameworks (NIST, ISO 27001, SOC 2), and data privacy laws (GDPR, HIPAA, CCPA) are consistently underestimated by technical candidates who focus almost entirely on attacks and defences. This domain is heavily tested and the content is learnable quickly. Don't skip it.

The study plan that works

1
Weeks 1‚Äì3 ‚Äî Threats, attacks, and cryptography
The highest-weight technical domains first

Start with threats and vulnerabilities ‚Äî this is the largest technical domain and the one where scenario practice pays off most. Learn how each attack works conceptually, not just its name. Then cover cryptography thoroughly: symmetric vs asymmetric, hashing, PKI, and digital signatures appear constantly across multiple domains.

Malware types ‚Äî virus, worm, trojan, ransomware, rootkit, spyware ‚Äî and how each spreads
Social engineering ‚Äî phishing, spear phishing, vishing, pretexting, tailgating
Network attacks ‚Äî MitM, DoS/DDoS, ARP poisoning, DNS spoofing, replay attacks
Cryptography ‚Äî symmetric (AES), asymmetric (RSA/ECC), hashing (SHA-256), PKI, digital signatures
Do 10‚Äì15 practice questions per topic as you go ‚Äî don't just read
2
Weeks 4‚Äì6 ‚Äî Architecture, IAM, and security operations
Zero Trust, identity management, SIEM, incident response

Security architecture covers how secure environments are designed ‚Äî Zero Trust, network segmentation, cloud security models, and resilience. Identity and access management is one of the most consistently tested areas: MFA, SSO, access control models (DAC/MAC/RBAC/ABAC), and privileged access. Incident response requires knowing the phases in order.

Zero Trust principles ‚Äî verify explicitly, least privilege, assume breach
IAM ‚Äî MFA factors, SSO, SAML, LDAP/Kerberos, access control models
Cloud security ‚Äî shared responsibility model, IaaS/PaaS/SaaS, CASB, cloud-native controls
Incident response phases ‚Äî preparation, detection, containment, eradication, recovery, lessons learned
SIEM ‚Äî log aggregation, correlation, alerting ‚Äî know what it does and when it's appropriate
3
Weeks 7‚Äì8 ‚Äî Governance, risk, and compliance
The 20% most candidates neglect

Governance is the domain technical people skip and then fail on. Risk management concepts, compliance frameworks, and data privacy regulations are straightforward to learn but require dedicated time. This material is also some of the fastest to get through ‚Äî it's mostly conceptual with no calculations or hands-on skills required.

Risk terminology ‚Äî risk, threat, vulnerability, likelihood, impact, risk appetite
Frameworks ‚Äî NIST CSF, ISO 27001, SOC 2, CIS Controls ‚Äî know what each is used for
Data privacy regulations ‚Äî GDPR, HIPAA, CCPA, PCI DSS ‚Äî scope and key requirements
Third-party risk ‚Äî vendor assessments, supply chain risk, right-to-audit clauses
Security policies ‚Äî AUP, data classification, change management, business continuity
4
Weeks 9‚Äì10 ‚Äî Practice exams and targeted review
Full timed tests, identify weak areas, book and sit the exam

Switch entirely to practice exams. Security+ scenario questions are longer and more complex than A+ or Network+ ‚Äî slow down and read every word of the scenario before looking at answers. For every wrong answer, understand the reasoning: Security+ questions often eliminate two options immediately but leave two plausible ones, and the distinction matters.

Take 3‚Äì4 full practice exams under timed 90-minute conditions
Score each domain separately ‚Äî target anything below 75%
Pay attention to PBQs ‚Äî practice drag-and-drop and matching question types
Book the exam when consistently hitting 80%+ on Dion Training or Professor Messer practice tests

Exam day tips

üö©
Flag PBQs and skip first
PBQs appear at the start and take significantly longer than multiple choice. Flag every PBQ immediately, skip to multiple choice, answer all you can, then return to PBQs with remaining time. This is the single most impactful time management strategy.
üîç
Identify the actual question
Security+ scenarios are long. Before reading the answers, identify exactly what is being asked ‚Äî "what type of attack is this?", "what should be done first?", "which control would prevent this?" Answering the wrong question is a common failure mode.
üóëÔ∏è
Eliminate first
On hard questions, eliminate the two clearly wrong answers first. You'll usually be left with two plausible options ‚Äî at that point read the scenario again carefully for the specific detail that distinguishes them. The answer is always in the scenario.
‚öñÔ∏è
"Best" means most appropriate
When the question asks for the "best" control or "most appropriate" response, multiple answers may be technically correct. The right answer is the one most proportionate to the scenario ‚Äî don't over-engineer simple situations.
‚è±Ô∏è
Budget your time carefully
90 minutes for up to 90 questions plus PBQs. Aim for 1 minute per multiple choice question maximum. Flag anything that takes more than 90 seconds and come back ‚Äî running out of time is a preventable failure mode.
üéØ
80%+ on practice tests = ready
Consistently hitting 80%+ on Dion Training or Professor Messer practice exams means you're ready. The real exam is similar in format and difficulty to quality practice tests. Don't delay booking out of anxiety.
‚ö° The most common reasons people fail Security+

Treating it like a memorisation exam. Security+ tests application and judgment, not recall. If you can recite definitions but can't work through a scenario to identify the attack type and best mitigation, the exam will expose that gap immediately.

Skipping the governance domain. Risk management, compliance frameworks, and data privacy laws make up 20% of the exam. Candidates with purely technical backgrounds often skip this material entirely and lose an avoidable 10‚Äì15 questions.

Not doing enough practice exams. The scenario format is a skill that improves with practice.

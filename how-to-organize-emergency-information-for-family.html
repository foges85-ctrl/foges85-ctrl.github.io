How to Organize Emergency Information for Your Family

A practical, step-by-step guide to gathering the documents, accounts, and passwords your loved ones will actually need — and storing them somewhere they can reach without you.

Quick Answer

Organize emergency information into five categories: identity documents, financial accounts, insurance and medical records, digital accounts and passwords, and final wishes. Store the physical originals in a fireproof safe, keep a digital backup encrypted behind a strong password or biometric lock, and tell at least one trusted person where to find both.

If something happened to you tomorrow, could the people you love find your bank accounts? Your will? The password to your phone? For most families, the honest answer is no — and the weeks after a death or medical emergency are already hard enough without a treasure hunt through old filing cabinets and sticky notes.

The good news is that organizing this information is a weekend project, not a lifetime one. This guide walks through exactly what to gather, how to structure it, and where to store it so your family has a clear path forward when they need it most.

Why Most People Put This Off (and Why You Shouldn't)

Nobody enjoys thinking about their own death or incapacitation. Estate planning feels like something older people do, or something that requires a lawyer and a lot of money. Neither is true. The real barriers are usually simpler: not knowing where to start, and not having a trusted place to keep sensitive information once you've gathered it.

The cost of not doing this falls entirely on your family. They'll spend weeks or months tracking down accounts, calling companies, guessing passwords, and searching your home. Some accounts may be lost entirely — dormant bank accounts get turned over to the state, subscriptions keep charging, and crypto wallets without recovery phrases are gone forever.

The point of an emergency information system isn't to plan for death. It's to give the people you love a clear map so they can focus on grieving, healing, or handling an emergency instead of playing detective.

The Five Categories of Emergency Information

Rather than thinking about this as one giant task, break it into five categories. Tackle one per weekend and you'll be done in just over a month.

1. Identity and Legal Documents

These are the foundational documents your family will need to prove who you are, settle your affairs, and make legal decisions on your behalf.

  • Birth certificate — required for death certificates and estate matters
  • Social Security card — needed for benefits claims and tax filings
  • Passport and driver's license — identification for account closures
  • Marriage certificate or divorce decree — affects inheritance and benefits
  • Military discharge papers (DD-214) — required for VA benefits
  • Will, living will, and healthcare proxy — directs end-of-life decisions
  • Power of attorney documents — authorizes someone to act on your behalf
  • Property deeds and vehicle titles — proves ownership

Keep originals in a fireproof home safe or a bank safe deposit box. Scanned copies go in your digital backup.

2. Financial Accounts

This is usually the largest category and the most painful one to reconstruct after the fact. For each account, note the institution, account number (last four digits is enough for your list), account type, and whether there's a designated beneficiary.

  • Checking and savings accounts
  • Retirement accounts (401k, IRA, Roth IRA)
  • Brokerage and investment accounts
  • Cryptocurrency wallets and exchange accounts
  • Pensions and annuities
  • Outstanding loans and mortgages
  • Credit cards (so recurring charges can be stopped)
  • Safe deposit boxes

Beneficiary tip: Retirement accounts and life insurance with named beneficiaries bypass your will entirely. Review these designations every few years — an ex-spouse listed on a 401k from 2015 will still inherit it in 2026 if you never updated it.

3. Insurance and Medical Records

Your family will need to file claims, continue coverage, and understand your medical history for their own planning.

  • Health insurance policy and card
  • Life insurance policies (term and whole life)
  • Disability and long-term care insurance
  • Homeowners or renters insurance
  • Auto insurance
  • List of doctors and specialists with contact info
  • Current medications and dosages
  • Allergies and major diagnoses
  • Organ donor status

4. Digital Accounts and Passwords

This category didn't exist a generation ago and is now often the hardest to handle. Your family may need access to email, cloud storage, social media, subscription services, and two-factor authentication apps — and most of these are protected by passwords they don't know and devices they can't unlock.

At minimum, document:

  • Device passcodes — phone, laptop, tablet, and any backup devices
  • Primary email account password — this is the master key to almost everything else
  • Password manager master password — if you use one (and you should)
  • Cloud storage accounts — iCloud, Google Drive, Dropbox
  • Social media accounts — with instructions on whether to memorialize or delete
  • Two-factor authentication recovery codes — often overlooked and critical
  • Cryptocurrency seed phrases — without these, the funds are permanently lost
  • Subscription services — so they can be cancelled to stop charges

A warning about writing passwords on paper: A sheet of paper in a drawer is a security risk while you're alive (burglary, snooping visitors, curious kids) and may not even be found after you're gone. You want something encrypted, accessible only through authentication, and easy for a trusted person to reach when they need to.

5. Final Wishes and Personal Instructions

This is the category most people skip, and it's the one your family will actually appreciate the most. It isn't legally binding the way a will is, but it fills in the gaps a will doesn't cover.

  • Funeral or memorial preferences (burial, cremation, specific wishes)
  • Whether you've pre-paid any funeral arrangements
  • Obituary preferences — what you'd want included or left out
  • People to notify (close friends, distant relatives, work contacts)
  • Pet care instructions
  • Letters or messages for specific family members
  • Items with sentimental value and who you'd want to have them

Where to Store All of This

You want three layers of storage, and each layer has a different purpose.

Layer 1: Physical Originals

Original documents — birth certificates, wills, property deeds, military records — belong in a fireproof, waterproof home safe. A quality safe costs $150–300 and is worth every dollar. A bank safe deposit box is also an option but has a major drawback: it may be sealed after your death until the estate is settled, which can take weeks.

Layer 2: Digital Backup

Scan every physical document and store the scans somewhere encrypted. Options include an encrypted folder on your computer, a password manager with document storage (1Password and Bitwarden both support this), or a dedicated emergency info app. The digital layer is what your family will actually use first, because they can access it from anywhere.

Layer 3: The Handoff

None of this matters if nobody knows it exists. Pick one or two trusted people — a spouse, a sibling, an adult child, or a close friend — and have a real conversation with them. Tell them:

  • Where the physical safe is and how to open it
  • What digital tool you're using and how to access it
  • Where your will is and who the executor is
  • Who your attorney is, if you have one

You don't have to share the actual passwords during this conversation. You just have to make sure the path to them is known.

A Weekend-by-Weekend Plan

Your Four-Weekend Checklist

  • Weekend 1: Gather identity and legal documents. Scan them. Buy a fireproof safe if you don't have one.
  • Weekend 2: List every financial account. Review beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance.
  • Weekend 3: Document insurance policies, medical info, and digital accounts. Set up a password manager or emergency info app if you haven't already.
  • Weekend 4: Write out final wishes and personal instructions. Have the handoff conversation with your trusted person.

How Often to Update

Treat this like a smoke detector — set a yearly reminder to review everything. Major life events (marriage, divorce, new child, new home, new job with new benefits) also trigger an update. The review itself usually takes less than an hour once the initial system is in place.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a lawyer to do this?

Not for the organizational work described here. You do need a lawyer (or a reputable online service like Trust & Will or LegalZoom) to draft a legally valid will, power of attorney, and healthcare directive. Once those documents exist, organizing them is a DIY task.

Is it safe to keep passwords digitally?

Yes, if you use a tool with strong encryption and biometric or master-password protection. A reputable password manager or a dedicated encrypted app is far safer than a paper list in a drawer or a notes file on your desktop. The risk of a well-designed encrypted tool is extremely low compared to the risk of your family losing access to critical accounts.

What about my social media accounts?

Most major platforms have legacy or memorialization options. Facebook lets you designate a legacy contact in your settings. Google has an Inactive Account Manager. Apple now supports Legacy Contacts for Apple IDs. Set these up while you're organizing everything else — it takes about ten minutes per platform.

What if I don't own much and don't have a complex estate?

You still benefit from this exercise. Even renters with simple finances have phones that need to be unlocked, subscriptions that need to be cancelled, photos that should be preserved, and people who should be notified. The financial complexity is only one piece of the puzzle.

How do I handle cryptocurrency?

Crypto is the single most common thing lost permanently after a death. If you hold crypto in a self-custody wallet, the seed phrase must be recoverable by a trusted person — otherwise the funds are gone forever. Write the seed phrase down, store it in your safe, and make sure your trusted person knows it exists. Exchange-held crypto (Coinbase, Kraken) is easier to recover through the estate but still requires login credentials and documentation.

Final Thoughts

Organizing emergency information isn't about dwelling on mortality. It's about taking care of the people you love by not leaving them a mess. A weekend of focused work now saves your family weeks of stress and tens of thousands of dollars in lost accounts, missed deadlines, and professional fees.

Start with whichever category feels easiest. Momentum matters more than perfection here. The worst version of this plan is still infinitely better than no plan at all.

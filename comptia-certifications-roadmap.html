The Short Answer — Which Cert First?

If you're new to IT with no experience: start with CompTIA A+. It's the industry-standard entry point, covers the hardware and OS fundamentals every other cert builds on, and is recognized by virtually every IT employer as proof of baseline competency.

If you already have help desk or networking experience: you can go straight to Network+. A+ is not a prerequisite for any CompTIA cert — it's a recommendation, not a requirement.

If your goal is cybersecurity specifically: the standard path is A+ → Network+ → Security+. Security+ is the most recognized entry-level cybersecurity certification and is DoD 8570 approved — required for many government and defense contractor roles.

CompTIA vs Cisco vs (ISC)² — which vendor?

CompTIA is vendor-neutral and ideal for generalists starting out — certs are widely recognised and don't expire (with CE continuing education). Best for IT support, entry cybersecurity, and broad IT roles.

Cisco (CCNA/CCNP) is vendor-specific to Cisco networking gear — essential if your organisation runs Cisco, highly valued in networking roles but less relevant in non-Cisco environments. CCNA is broadly regarded as harder than Network+.

(ISC)² (CISSP) is the gold standard for senior security professionals — requires 5 years of security experience, extremely comprehensive, and commands the highest salaries in cybersecurity. Not appropriate as a starting point.

For most people entering IT: start with CompTIA. Add Cisco or (ISC)² credentials later as your career specialises.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need A+ before Network+?

No — A+ is not a prerequisite for Network+. CompTIA recommends it, but it's not enforced. If you already have IT experience or a strong understanding of computer hardware and operating systems, you can go straight to Network+. If you're completely new to IT, A+ is the better starting point.

How long does each cert take to prepare for?

A+: 2–4 months studying 1–2 hours per day for most beginners. Network+: 2–3 months with some networking background. Security+: 2–3 months after Network+. These are rough averages — people with relevant experience can pass significantly faster; those starting from zero may take longer.

Do CompTIA certifications expire?

Yes — CompTIA certifications are valid for 3 years. You renew through CompTIA's CE (Continuing Education) program by earning continuing education units (CEUs), passing a higher-level exam, or retaking the current exam. Higher-level cert renewals often cascade down — renewing Security+ renews Network+ and A+ as well.

Is Security+ worth it without experience?

Security+ is harder without hands-on experience, but many people pass it by studying hard. The real question is whether the cert alone will get you a job — and the honest answer is that Security+ combined with a home lab, practical skills, and even A+ is a much stronger package than Security+ alone. Use the cert as a target, not a substitute for skills.

How Long Each Certification Takes to Prepare For

Study time varies significantly based on prior experience, but realistic estimates help with planning. CompTIA A+ requires 8–12 weeks for candidates without IT background, studying 1–2 hours daily across both Core 1 and Core 2 exams. Candidates with help desk experience can often compress this to 4–6 weeks. CompTIA Network+ requires 6–10 weeks for candidates who have A+ or equivalent experience, and up to 14 weeks for those without networking background. CompTIA Security+ requires 8–12 weeks for candidates with Network+ experience; those without networking background may need 12–16 weeks because approximately 30% of Security+ content assumes networking knowledge.

These estimates assume a focused, daily study approach — not weekend-only cramming. Candidates who try to compress preparation into 2–3 weeks consistently report struggling with the scenario-based question format, which requires time for conceptual understanding to develop. Book your exam date only after you're consistently scoring 80–85%+ on full-length practice exams.

Certification Costs — What to Budget

CompTIA exam vouchers are purchased separately from study materials. Current prices (2025): A+ Core 1: $246, A+ Core 2: $246 (total $492 for both A+ exams), Network+: $369, Security+: $392. Full A+, Network+, and Security+ path: approximately $1,253 in exam fees alone before study materials.

Study material costs vary widely. Professor Messer offers free video courses for all three certifications — a serious, viable option for cost-conscious candidates. Paid resources like Dion Training practice exams ($30–50 per exam pack) and study guides ($40–60) significantly improve pass rates, particularly for the scenario-based questions that free resources cover less thoroughly. Many employers reimburse certification costs — check your employment benefits before paying out of pocket.

CompTIA offers a Retake Policy: if you fail, you can retake the exam. The first retake requires a 14-day waiting period. A second failure requires a 14-day wait and restricts you to the same vendor for 12 months for that exam. Factor potential retake costs into your budget — another reason to over-prepare rather than sit early.

After Security+ — Where to Go Next

Security+ is not the ceiling of the CompTIA certification path. From Security+, the natural next steps depend on your career direction. CySA+ (CompTIA Cybersecurity Analyst) focuses on threat detection, security monitoring, and incident response — the analytical side of security operations. PenTest+ covers penetration testing and vulnerability assessment. CASP+ (CompTIA Advanced Security Practitioner) is an advanced certification for senior security practitioners and architects.

Outside CompTIA, common next steps include Certified Ethical Hacker (CEH) from EC-Council for offensive security focus, CISSP (Certified Information Systems Security Professional) from ISC2 for senior security management roles (requires 5 years of experience), and cloud-specific certifications like AWS Solutions Architect or Microsoft Azure Administrator for cloud security and infrastructure roles.

The A+ → Network+ → Security+ path is not the only valid route — it's simply the most common and most structured entry point. Some candidates go directly to cloud certifications or skip Network+ if their background is specifically in security rather than general networking. The right path depends on where you want to work and what the job postings in your target roles actually list as requirements.

Is the A+ to Security+ Path Worth It?

The short answer is yes for most people entering IT from a non-technical background. The three certifications provide a structured, vendor-neutral foundation that employers recognize and that genuinely teaches the knowledge you need to do entry-level IT and security work. They are not shortcuts — candidates who pass A+, Network+, and Security+ have legitimately studied and applied networking and security fundamentals.

The limitations: certifications alone, without hands-on experience, are a floor rather than a ceiling. Employers in competitive markets are increasingly looking for certifications plus a home lab, a GitHub portfolio, documented projects, or help desk experience. Building a home lab (even a simple one with VirtualBox or VMware running a few virtual machines) while studying dramatically improves both your exam performance and your employability, because you can speak to real-world application in interviews.
